Behavioural Guide

The below sections demonstrates my key behavioural design considerations for links.

The link's clickability is clearly portrayed through it's distinct hover effect. The hover effect gives instant feedback so the user is aware of the link. The typical 'Facebook blue' also provides familiarity to users and will stand out to any user who is actively looking for a facebook link.

The language used in the link makes it clear that this is link to the groups facebook page.

Links will include a tabindex to ensure they are keyboard accesible.

This section explains my key behavioural design considerations for my menu and navigation bars.

All icons have equal visual weight with a white background and orange icon. In the hover example, the home icon has an orange background creating a clear visual demonstration of the selected state.

The left to right arrangment flows in a natural order of priority. The consistent positioning of icons supports spatial memory and creates a sense of continuity and familiarity for users.

This section explains my main design considerations for the implementations of cards in my website.

My cards will exhibit a simple and familiar design. The colour scheme aligns with the overall website. A clear defined border means they are easily discoverable.

Distinct title and information sections are applied in a meaningful sequence to allow for easy access to desired information. Simple icons will be optionally added to cards to help identify the information on the card. Cards throughout the page will be orderd in a meaningful sequence to help assist the navigation of information.

This section explains my main design considerations for the implementations of icons in my website.

Icons exhibit labels to improve both discoverability and clarity. The icon remains the main feature, however, the label provides clarity and through the use of aria-labels supports screen reader use. This helps to keep in line with WCAG guidelines.

The icons all maintain a simple design to help reduce cognitive load on users and provide familiar context to help build trust.

This section explains my main design considerations for the implementations of forms in my website.

The form is layed out in logical grouping displayed in a vertical stack. This is how people typically view forms and provides familiarity with ease of use.

Each input section is clearly paired with a descriptive label, aligning with WCAG 3.3.2. This makes it obvious what information is needed.

Additional information is provided when required. This makes it clear what is required from the user, further consolidating the forms alliance with WCAG 3.3.2.

The input boxes provide clear and immediate feedback when selected and typed in. This aligns with Don Norman's principles and helps build trust with the user. Furthermore, when required, extra feedback messages are displayed on the screen. This further ensures there is no confusion on the users end.
